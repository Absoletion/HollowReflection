# Hollow Reflections — Art-Direction Bible

**Version:** 1.0  
**Primary benchmark:** `Hale-4star-reference.png`  
**Benchmark use:** Hale’s proposed 4★ form and the visual north star for the game—not a universal purple palette.

## 1. Art-direction statement

**Hollow Reflections is a vibrant gothic-anime fantasy where precise, ornate character designs are cut from deep neutral silhouettes and ignited by concentrated elemental color.** Characters should feel heroic and collectible at a glance, richly constructed at portrait scale, and readable in motion at combat scale. The world is dark because of material, atmosphere, and story—not because the finished images are muddy or desaturated.

The target is a **premium illustrated 2.5D anime presentation**:

- High-detail key art for summons, library pages, evolutions, and major story moments.
- Simplified but faithful layered character art for battle animation.
- Painted environments with parallax-ready depth layers.
- Dark, restrained UI framing that lets character and elemental color remain dominant.
- Live VFX that use the same shape language as the character’s equipment and iconography.

## 2. Five visual pillars

### 2.1 Readable heroic silhouette

Every unit must be recognizable in solid black at thumbnail size. Use one dominant silhouette idea and no more than two supporting ideas.

- Hale 4★: swept hair + raised armored collar + asymmetric shoulder plate + long cape + oversized crystalline sword.
- Weapons, coats, capes, hats, shields, and hairstyles should identify a unit before facial details do.
- Effects may frame a silhouette but must not replace it.

### 2.2 Ornate focal detail

Detail is concentrated at the face, upper torso, signature weapon, and one secondary story-bearing area. It should thin out toward less important surfaces.

- Use layered plates, buckles, bevels, etched motifs, stitched seams, and controlled filigree.
- Repeat a unit’s motif at three scales: emblem, equipment detail, and VFX geometry.
- Avoid evenly distributing noise across the entire costume.

### 2.3 Dark neutrals, luminous color

Approximately **65% neutral structure / 25% material and secondary color / 10% elemental emission**.

- “Dark” comes from obsidian, charcoal, ink-plum, smoked steel, leather, and deep fabric.
- Element colors should reach bright, near-white cores in small areas.
- Never tint every surface with the element color. Saturation is a reward for the eye.

### 2.4 Sharp gothic geometry

Use crescents, thorns, cathedral circles, blades, fractured glass, pointed arches, and four-point stars. Curves should usually terminate in a deliberate point.

- Friendly designs can soften the geometry with rounder spacing and broader shapes.
- Threatening or Hollowed designs use inward hooks, broken symmetry, missing segments, and unstable repetition.
- Sacred and institutional designs use complete circles, measured symmetry, and orderly divisions.

### 2.5 Controlled spectacle

Key art may be lavish, but the face, hands, weapon action, and pose must remain legible.

- One primary action line.
- One dominant effect arc.
- One graphic halo, crest, or environmental framing device.
- Small particles support motion; they do not fill every empty space.

## 3. Character rendering standard

### Proportions and anatomy

- Stylized young-adult anime proportions, generally 6.5–7.25 heads tall when standing.
- Faces use a tapered jaw, compact nose and mouth, large but not childlike eyes, and clearly shaped upper lashes.
- Hands must be posed intentionally and remain readable; avoid hiding both hands without narrative reason.
- Action poses favor a lowered center of gravity, strong foreshortening, and a clear line from gaze through weapon or casting hand.
- Preserve believable armor articulation at shoulders, elbows, hips, knees, and ankles.

### Linework

- Clean, tapered linework with dark plum-black rather than featureless pure black.
- Strongest contour at the outer silhouette and overlapping forms.
- Thinner lines on faces, hair interiors, reflected edges, and luminous surfaces.
- Small metal details may use painted edges instead of outlining every mark.

### Shading

Use a cel-painted hybrid:

1. Local base color.
2. One broad, shape-defining shadow.
3. A smaller occlusion shadow at overlaps and recesses.
4. Material-specific highlights.
5. Selective colored rim light from elemental effects.

Do not airbrush all transitions. Soft rendering is reserved for skin, atmospheric light, glow falloff, and selected fabric folds. Armor, hair clumps, weapons, and crystalline effects stay crisp.

### Faces and expressions

- Eyes are the emotional focal point and must stay unobstructed in standard portraits.
- Catchlights inherit a small amount of the scene or element color.
- Neutral expressions should still communicate temperament.
- Each production-ready unit requires: neutral, confident, concerned, angry, hurt, and warm/amused expressions.
- Villains and Hollowed creatures should remain expressive without relying only on glowing eyes.

### Hair

- Construct hair from large directional masses, medium locks, then a small number of fine strands.
- Use broad shadow grouping; avoid rendering every strand equally.
- Add a narrow cool or element-colored rim where effects strike the silhouette.
- Hair highlights should follow clump direction rather than forming a generic horizontal band.

## 4. Material language

| Material | Rendering rule | Highlight character |
|---|---|---|
| Polished steel | Hard plane changes, dark reflected bands, bright narrow edges | White-silver with cool violet/blue bounce |
| Blackened steel | Near-black body with readable gray planes and damaged edges | Sparse, sharp, never uniformly black |
| Leather | Matte-to-satin, warm dark brown, visible edge wear | Broad low-intensity highlight |
| Heavy fabric | Large folds, soft value transitions, limited specular response | Color appears in turning planes |
| Silk or lining | Cleaner folds and stronger directional sheen | Saturated secondary color |
| Hollowglass | Dark or translucent body, bright internal fissures, razor edges | Near-white core fading to element hue |
| Skin | Warm local color, soft cheek/nose transitions, crisp cast shadows | Minimal bloom; preserve facial structure |

Every costume should visibly contain at least three material families. Material separation is more important than adding another layer of ornament.

## 5. Color system

### Hale 4★ benchmark palette

| Function | Color | Hex starting point |
|---|---|---|
| Deepest contour | Obsidian Ink | `#12101C` |
| Dark fabric | Ink Plum | `#21152E` |
| Dark-element body | Void Violet | `#51247F` |
| Active energy | Arc Violet | `#914AF2` |
| Energy core | Violet Flare | `#DFC4FF` |
| Blackened metal | Smoked Steel | `#484854` |
| Mid metal | Forged Silver | `#9999A7` |
| Bright metal/hair | Moon Silver | `#ECEAF0` |
| Warm leather | Umber Strap | `#3B2925` |
| Skin midtone | Warm Sand | `#E2AE8D` |
| Eye/story accent | Scar Red | `#D7373F` |

These are working anchors, not eyedropper mandates. Final colors should be adjusted for neighboring values, lighting, and accessibility.

### Element accent families

The neutral material framework stays consistent; each element replaces the energy, rim-light, and selected lining/accent colors.

| Element | Shadow accent | Main emission | Hot core | Shape tendency |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Fire | Oxblood | Vermilion-orange | Yellow-white | Tongues, wedges, rising sparks |
| Water | Deep cobalt | Cyan-azure | Ice white | Ribbons, droplets, concentric curves |
| Earth | Forest teal | Emerald-jade | Yellow-green | Leaves, facets, rooted polygons |
| Thunder | Indigo | Electric yellow | White | Zigzags, forks, sudden straight cuts |
| Light | Warm umber | Gold-ivory | Pure white | Complete rings, rays, ordered stars |
| Dark | Ink plum | Violet-magenta | Pale lavender | Crescents, thorns, devouring arcs |
| Hollow | Blue-gray | Glass teal with lilac fringe | Colorless white | Cracks, missing segments, refracted shards |

**Important distinction:** Dark is saturated, shadow-fed, and organic. Hollow is pale, refractive, incomplete, and unnaturally quiet. If Hale remains mechanically Hollow rather than Dark, his 4★ effects should introduce glass-teal or colorless refraction inside the violet shell so the two identities do not collapse into one.

### Contrast rule

- Keep the face in a clear mid-to-light value pocket.
- Place the darkest dark and brightest light near the focal action.
- Do not put full-strength glow behind the entire silhouette.
- In battle, unit, enemy, and ground values must separate without relying on outlines alone.

## 6. Hale 4★ canonical visual brief

### Preserve from the benchmark

- Tousled moon-silver hair with cool gray-violet shadow.
- Red eyes as a persistent story accent rather than the elemental emission color.
- Young, controlled expression: capable but not cruel.
- High armored collar and asymmetric upper-body armor.
- Layered blackened-steel and polished-silver plate over dark fabric and leather harnesses.
- Deep plum-black cape with a heraldic inner or outer motif.
- Oversized sword combining silver blade geometry with a violet-black Hollowglass core.
- Circular cathedral/sigil framing and orbiting blade-star shapes.
- Neck scar or mark retained as a story identifier.
- Forward-reaching off hand, communicating command over the arsenal.

### Clarify in the production model

- Establish one definitive sword design; the key art must not contain ambiguous duplicate blade structures.
- Simplify small belt and torso details for the battle rig while retaining their rhythm.
- Convert the background halo into a separate effect asset, not part of Hale’s body rig.
- Define the cape emblem as clean vector reference before repainting it.
- Ensure the armor has consistent hinges and attachment points from front, side, and back.

### 3★ → 4★ evolution logic

Hale’s 3★ design should contain the same identity in a restrained form:

- Silver hair, red eyes, neck mark, high collar, and one recognizable sword motif remain.
- Less plate coverage; more practical guild clothing and worn leather.
- One incomplete shoulder guard rather than the full asymmetric plate assembly.
- A simpler steel sword with only a narrow internal Hollowglass seam.
- Shorter cape or split coat tails.
- Effects use fractured lines and isolated shards rather than a complete cathedral circle.

The 4★ story evolution completes existing ideas. It should feel revealed—not as if Hale changed into an unrelated character.

### Future 5★ boundary

Do not design 5★ merely by adding larger wings, more belts, or more particles. Reserve at least two major silhouette changes and one new visual law for it. Possible directions remain intentionally open: liberated arsenal, inverted Hollowglass, royal/garrison symbolism, or a cost made visible.

## 7. Rarity and evolution language

| Rarity | Visual density | Silhouette | Effects | Presentation |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1★–2★ | Simple, practical | One clear identifier | Minimal accent | Clean card, little framing |
| 3★ | Established adventurer | Signature weapon/clothing | One controlled aura or trail | Basic crest and rarity frame |
| 4★ | Heroic, story-capable | Secondary silhouette feature | Motif circle, orbit, or field effect | Full illustrated key art |
| 5★ | Mythic or awakened | Meaningful transformation | Environment-reactive signature effect | Unique reveal and Burst treatment |

Rarity changes should alter story, silhouette, and material—not only ornament count.

## 8. Weapons, armor, and motifs

- Design weapons at thumbnail size first; internal engraving is secondary.
- Repeat one family of angles between weapon, armor, and effects.
- Oversized weapons are allowed, but the grip, balance, and action path must read.
- Armor should frame motion rather than turn every unit into the same knight.
- Each faction receives a construction grammar: edge profile, fastening method, emblem placement, and favored materials.
- Damage and wear are localized to story-useful areas; avoid generic scratches everywhere.

## 9. VFX language

Every major effect uses three readable layers:

1. **Core:** small, hottest, fastest shape showing the exact hit or activation point.
2. **Body:** the element’s recognizable arc, field, trail, or sigil.
3. **Debris:** sparks, droplets, leaves, fragments, motes, or distortion that describe force and decay.

### Timing

- Skill: immediate silhouette snap, short trail, fast decay.
- Art: brief anticipation, motif reveal, clear impact, lingering identity particles.
- Burst: character cut-in, large signature geometry, staged impact, controlled return to play.
- Defensive effect: broad stable shapes and outward-facing arcs.
- Healing/support: rising motion, breathing rhythm, and open shapes rather than impact spikes.

Avoid constant full-screen bloom. White cores should occupy a small percentage of the frame so Burst brightness still feels exceptional.

## 10. Environment direction

The world uses **painted gothic fantasy grounded by lived-in materials**.

- Architecture: pointed roofs, arches, vertical banners, stone circles, ironwork, carved guild emblems.
- Town warmth: amber windows, weathered timber, cloth awnings, food smoke, worn cobbles, practical clutter.
- Hollowing intrusion: reflected light without a source, incomplete circles, glass growth, silent particles, wrong-value edges.
- Nature remains colorful; corruption should contrast with it rather than turning every location gray.

### Background construction

Each major environment should be delivered in at least five separable layers:

1. Sky/atmosphere.
2. Far architecture or landscape.
3. Main playable structures.
4. Foreground framing.
5. Light, fog, particles, and corruption overlays.

Town locations should be recognizable by silhouette and lighting even with all labels removed.

## 11. UI direction

UI serves as a dark gallery frame around vibrant art.

- Base: blue-black lacquer and charcoal panels.
- Structure: thin smoked-steel or antique-silver borders.
- Premium/story accent: restrained warm gold.
- Active combat accent: unit element color.
- Hollow-specific UI: fractured glass edges and incomplete rings, used sparingly.
- Buttons use broad readable faces; ornament belongs on corners and dividers, not under labels.
- Avoid pure-white panels, excessive gradients, and purple on every screen.
- Use 9-slice frames so decorative borders scale without distortion.

Typography should pair a readable modern sans serif for systems with a restrained high-contrast serif for chapter titles, rarity reveals, and lore. Never sacrifice mobile legibility for ornament.

## 12. Composition standards by asset type

### Summon/library key art

- Square master, dynamic three-quarter action pose.
- Face within the upper-middle focal region.
- Weapon and effect arcs remain inside crop-safe margins.
- Transparent character/effect version plus composed showcase version.
- Background motif must be separable.

### Dialogue portrait

- Chest-up or waist-up.
- Neutral three-quarter base pose with swappable expressions.
- Hair and costume silhouette must survive narrow mobile crops.
- Keep mouth, eyes, brows, and optional hand gesture separable where feasible.

### Battle model

- Prioritize clean limbs, weapon path, face, and silhouette over key-art microdetail.
- Reduce fine buckles, engraving, and cloth noise by roughly 40–60%.
- Preserve material blocks, signature motifs, major armor layers, and dominant colors.
- Effects and ground shadows are never baked into the body art.

### Arts/Burst cut-in

- Wide diagonal composition.
- Face, eyes, casting hand, and weapon must read in under half a second.
- Reserve a clean region for the Art/Burst name.
- Use a bespoke motif arrangement rather than cropping the library illustration.

## 13. Technical asset standard

| Asset | Recommended master | Delivery |
|---|---:|---|
| Library/summon illustration | 2400 × 2400 px minimum | Layered PSD/CLIP/KRA + transparent PNG + composed WebP |
| Dialogue bust | 1600 × 2200 px minimum | Layered source + transparent PNG/WebP |
| Battle rig | 2400–3200 px character height | Layered source with named body parts + atlas/export data |
| Burst cut-in | 2560 × 1440 px | Layered source + transparent foreground/effect layers |
| Environment | 2560 × 1440 px minimum | Layered source + separate parallax WebP/PNG layers |
| Unit/card thumbnail | 512 × 512 px | WebP plus PNG fallback if transparency requires it |
| UI icons | 256 × 256 px master | SVG where geometric; PNG/WebP where painted |
| VFX textures | Power-of-two where practical | Transparent PNG/WebP atlas or optimized sequence |

### Naming

Use lowercase stable identifiers:

`unit_form_asset_variant.ext`

Examples:

- `hale_4star_library_main.webp`
- `hale_4star_battle_body.png`
- `hale_4star_fx_cathedral-ring.png`
- `hale_4star_burst_cut-in.webp`
- `town_inn_bg_mid.webp`

Do not use final filenames such as `new`, `latest`, `fixed2`, or artist-local layer names.

### Layer naming for rigs

At minimum: head, face, front hair, rear hair, torso, pelvis, upper/lower arms, hands, upper/lower legs, feet, front/rear clothing, cape segments, weapon parts, and accessories. Left/right are named from the character’s perspective. Effects, halo, shadow, and UI are separate asset groups.

## 14. Mobile readability and performance

- Judge every character at full illustration size, dialogue size, battle size, and 96 px thumbnail size.
- Keep facial features and signature colors readable at approximately 140–220 px battle height.
- Maintain at least 8–10% composition-safe margin for device crops and UI overlays.
- Load large illustrations only on screens that use them.
- Use WebP for composed artwork where quality is acceptable; retain source PNGs and layered masters outside the shipped build.
- Atlas battle parts and VFX to reduce requests.
- Do not solve performance by shrinking master artwork; create controlled runtime exports.

## 15. Commission and provenance requirements

For any asset that may enter a marketed build, record:

- Artist or source.
- Date and version.
- Commercial-use and modification rights.
- Whether layered source files are included.
- Whether animation/rigging rights are included.
- Whether the artist permits promotional and store-page use.
- Any attribution requirement.
- Reference materials supplied to the artist.
- Approval history and requested corrections.

AI-assisted concepts may be used for exploration, but production assets need a documented, commercially safe origin and a human consistency pass. Do not build the final roster from unrelated one-off generations without a shared model sheet and revision process.

## 16. Review checklist

An asset is approved only when all applicable answers are “yes.”

### Identity

- Is the unit recognizable in silhouette and at thumbnail size?
- Are the established hair, face, story marks, equipment, and motif preserved?
- Does the design communicate role and temperament before reading text?

### Rendering

- Are materials visually distinct?
- Are the face and action readable before ornamental detail?
- Is elemental saturation concentrated rather than spread everywhere?
- Do the darkest dark and brightest light support the focal point?

### Game integration

- Does it crop safely in its intended screen?
- Can battle parts move without exposing gaps?
- Are VFX, shadows, and backgrounds separated?
- Are filenames, dimensions, and layers compliant?
- Does the asset remain readable on a phone-sized display?

### Cohesion

- Does it obey the five visual pillars?
- Does it share the world’s shape and material language without copying Hale’s palette?
- Does its rarity feel earned through silhouette and story, not just particle density?

## 17. Prohibited drift

Avoid:

- Making every character black, silver, and purple because Hale is the benchmark.
- Equal detail density across every costume surface.
- Muddy low-contrast “dark fantasy” rendering.
- Generic neon aura without element-specific geometry.
- Effects that cover the face or obscure combat timing.
- Inconsistent anatomy between a unit’s portrait, key art, and battle form.
- Random belts, buckles, wings, spikes, or halos added only to imply rarity.
- Flat materials that make steel, leather, cloth, and glass look identical.
- Modern minimalist UI that visually disconnects from the gothic world.
- Over-ornamented UI that competes with collectible character art.

## 19. Decisions and remaining questions

### Resolved

- **Hale's visual classification:** Dark with Hollow influence. Dark supplies approximately 80% of his color, motion, and attack geometry; Hollow supplies the pale refraction, suspended shards, and incomplete structures inside it.
- **Gameplay continuity:** this visual decision does not silently rewrite his existing Hollow gameplay tag or unique Hollowglass mechanics. A mechanical element change requires a separate systems decision.

### Remaining questions

- Is the red neck mark a scar, sigil, wound, or transformation indicator?
- Which cape emblem is canonical, and what faction or power does it represent?
- Does the cathedral circle belong to Hale, Hollowglass, awakening, or the wider setting?
- How much of the silver armor exists physically before awakening?
- What visual feature is being deliberately reserved for Hale’s future 5★ form?

Until answered, these features should remain editable and should not be propagated across other characters as universal lore.
